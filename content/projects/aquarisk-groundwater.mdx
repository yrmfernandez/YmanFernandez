## Context

Testing every groundwater well in a lab is slow and expensive. AquaRisk is an
AI-powered web app that helps **triage** wells instead: given water-chemistry
inputs, it estimates risk so that the samples most likely to be unsafe get sent
for laboratory confirmation first, rather than testing everything blindly.

It works from 1,106 post-monsoon samples spanning 33 districts of Telangana,
India (2018–2020), and produces three separate assessments.

## Approach

I built three models, each answering a different question:

- **Drinking safety** — an XGBoost classifier (Safe / Moderate / High risk),
  scored against Indian drinking-water standards.
- **Irrigation suitability** — a Random Forest over ion chemistry, using the USSL
  classification.
- **Anomaly detection** — an unsupervised Isolation Forest to flag unusual
  samples that don't fit the normal distribution.

Two decisions mattered most. First, I **deliberately withheld the labeled
features** (fluoride, nitrate, TDS, pH, and similar) from the drinking model, so
it had to learn from the surrounding chemistry rather than memorize the answer.
Second, I scored on **Macro-F1 rather than accuracy**, because the risk classes
are heavily imbalanced and accuracy would have rewarded ignoring the rare,
dangerous cases.

## Result

The drinking-safety model reached a **Macro-F1 of 0.670** and the irrigation model
**0.735**, with the anomaly detector surfacing outliers for review. Just as
important, the app is explicit that these are **estimates** — it warns that
consequential decisions still require lab confirmation, which keeps a screening
tool honest about what it can and can't promise.

## Reflection

The withheld-feature setup was the right call for integrity but leaves accuracy on
the table; a version that ingests certified lab readings where available could
serve both triage and confirmation. I'd also add calibrated probabilities so the
"test this well first" ranking reflects real confidence, not just a class label.
