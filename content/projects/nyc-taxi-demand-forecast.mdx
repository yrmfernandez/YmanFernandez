## Context

Fleet operators reposition vehicles ahead of demand, but they were working off
last week's averages — blind to weather, holidays, and local events. I built an
end-to-end pipeline that ingests raw trip records, aggregates them to hourly
demand per zone, and forecasts the next 24 hours.

The emphasis was as much on the **data engineering** as the model: the forecast
is only useful if it runs unattended every day on fresh data.

## Approach

I split the problem into ingestion and modeling.

- **Ingestion** — an Airflow DAG pulls the daily trip dump, validates schema and
  row counts, and loads hourly aggregates into PostgreSQL. Bad loads fail loudly
  rather than silently corrupting the training table.
- **Modeling** — began with a seasonal-naive baseline (predict last week, same
  hour), then SARIMA to capture daily and weekly seasonality, adding calendar
  and weather features as exogenous regressors.

The seasonal-naive baseline was a deliberate choice: it set a bar the "real"
model had to clear, and it turned out to be surprisingly strong on ordinary days.

## Result

The final model forecasts hourly demand within **8.1% MAPE** across a 24-hour
horizon — a clear improvement over the 13% baseline, with the biggest gains on
event and holiday days where the naive method failed badly.

## Reflection

The pipeline is the part I'm most proud of and also where I'd invest next: adding
data-quality tests (row-count deltas, null-rate checks) as first-class Airflow
tasks, and backfilling a longer history to model annual seasonality, which a few
months of data can't capture.
