## Context

A telecom provider was losing subscribers faster than its retention team could
react. The existing rule-based flag ("no usage for 30 days") caught churners far
too late to intervene. The goal was a model that surfaces at-risk customers
early enough to act, using a public dataset of ~40,000 accounts with usage,
billing, and support-contact features.

The hard constraint was **class imbalance** — only ~14% of accounts churned — and
a business preference to catch churners even at the cost of some false alarms.

## Approach

I framed success around **recall on the minority class**, not raw accuracy, since
a model that predicts "no churn" for everyone scores 86% accuracy and is useless.

- Started with logistic regression as an interpretable baseline (AUC 0.82).
- Engineered features around *trend* rather than *state*: month-over-month change
  in usage, rolling support-ticket counts, and tenure buckets.
- Moved to XGBoost and tuned with stratified cross-validation, using
  `scale_pos_weight` to counter the imbalance.
- Calibrated probabilities with isotonic regression so the retention team could
  set an intervention threshold they trusted.

What didn't work: aggressive SMOTE oversampling inflated validation scores but
generalized poorly on the held-out set, so I dropped it in favor of class
weighting.

## Result

The calibrated XGBoost model reached **AUC 0.91** and lifted minority-class
recall from 0.65 to 0.86 — a **32% reduction in missed churners** versus the
logistic baseline, at a threshold the business chose on the calibration curve.

## Reflection

With more time I'd validate on a true out-of-time split rather than a random
split, since churn behavior drifts seasonally. I'd also instrument the
intervention step to measure *lift on retained revenue*, which is the metric the
business ultimately cares about — model recall is only a proxy for it.
