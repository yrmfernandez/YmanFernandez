## Context

Dehydration is one of the hardest problems to catch early in pig farming. Farmers
usually rely on visible symptoms, but by the time those appear the animal may
already be seriously dehydrated — hurting growth, raising mortality risk, and
causing real economic loss for backyard farmers with limited access to
veterinarians.

PIGSaD (Pig Infrared-based Guided System for Automated Dehydration Detection) was
my undergraduate thesis: an AI-powered Android application that screens for
dehydration from **thermal images** instead of visual observation, and runs
**fully offline** once the model is on the device.

## Approach

When pigs become dehydrated, blood circulation changes and surface temperature
shifts around regions like the ear base and eye area — often *before* visible
clinical signs. A thermal camera can pick this up, so I framed the problem as
classifying a thermal image into **Normal**, **Possible Dehydration**, or
**Dehydrated**.

- **Data** — collected 1,934 thermal images from 15 pigs at a backyard farm in
  Calinan, Davao City. Each image was labeled into one of three classes using a
  Dehydration Index (DI) grounded in veterinary literature.
- **Experiments, Stage 1** — compared three methods of handling class imbalance
  to find the best training set.
- **Experiments, Stage 2** — compared four feature-engineering approaches: raw
  thermal images, ROI extraction, ROI + CLAHE enhancement, and thermal
  statistical features.
- Used an AutoML workflow so the system could search preprocessing and modeling
  choices rather than hand-tuning a single pipeline.

## Result

The **raw baseline** — unmodified thermal images — outperformed the more elaborate
feature-engineering approaches, a useful reminder that added complexity has to
earn its place. The best model used a **ResNet18** backbone and reached roughly
**61.3% accuracy** with the highest Macro-F1 among the approaches tested.

The trained model was exported to **ONNX** and successfully deployed on an Android
device for offline inference — closing the loop from data collection to a tool a
farmer could actually hold.

## Reflection

The headline accuracy has clear room to grow, and the honest next steps are about
data: more pigs, more farms, and more balanced classes would matter more than a
fancier model. I'd also validate on animals and conditions never seen in
training, since generalization — not leaderboard accuracy — is what decides
whether a screening tool is trustworthy in the field.
